## Abstract
Financial resilience concerns the rate at which a position recovers, or further deteriorates, in response to adverse conditions. Prior work introduced the resilience rate as the expected instantaneous rate of favorable change of a price or risk-assessment process, but this captures only the conditional mean of future increments and cannot distinguish positions with the same expected recovery but different conditional risk profiles. This paper obtains a richer characterization by evaluating such increments through a genuine, possibly nonlinear, dynamic risk measure: for an Itô process and a normalized, cash-additive dynamic risk measure, the resilience evaluation is defined via a specific limit. When the risk measure is convex and induced by a BSDE with a Lipschitz or quadratic driver, this limit is well-posed and admits an explicit dual representation as the worst-case conditional expectation, over a zero-penalty class of measure changes, of an effective drift combining the process's drift with the risk adjustment assigned to its volatility. The paper establishes attainment of the optimal scenario and illustrates the construction through examples and counterexamples.

## Abstract
This paper studies how a risk holder should combine self-protection and self-insurance when market insurance is absent. In a Bernoulli loss model, self-protection reduces the residual loss probability while self-insurance reduces the residual loss severity, and the risk holder evaluates residual risk using either Value-at-Risk or Tail Value-at-Risk while incurring a joint risk-reduction cost that allows technological interaction between the two activities. Value-at-Risk leads to a threshold-driven solution where the optimal strategy is either no risk reduction, pure self-protection, or pure self-insurance; Tail Value-at-Risk instead creates a direct interaction between residual frequency and severity, making the problem non-convex even in the Bernoulli setting. The paper solves this via an isoquant geometry method based on marginal-balance curves, identifying when optimal strategies lie on boundaries, extreme constrained candidates, touching components, or crossing components, and showing how the confidence level and cost technology determine whether self-protection and self-insurance behave as substitutes or complements.

## Abstract
Modern resolution and prudential regimes increasingly wind up a distressed firm not at a single hard threshold but through a graduated, state-dependent process. This paper studies how the design of such a regime shapes the trade-off between shareholder value and financial stability for a firm whose surplus follows a general diffusion, modeling forced liquidation in reduced form as arriving at a surplus-dependent hazard rate that rises as the firm's position deteriorates. The framework has three regions -- unregulated (dividends may be paid), regulated (solvency requirements prohibit distributions), and distress (facing the liquidation hazard) -- and the authors solve the resulting singular stochastic control problem of maximising expected present value of distributions until liquidation, establishing a verification theorem, proving a barrier strategy is optimal, and obtaining tractable expressions for the value function and expected survival time. They show a distress region placed solely below or solely above the classical ruin threshold does not consistently improve both shareholder value and firm survival, whereas combining the two yields a Pareto improvement -- regulatory design is decisive.

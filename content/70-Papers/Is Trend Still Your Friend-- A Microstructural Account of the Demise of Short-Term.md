## Abstract
Systematic trend following has, on average, been profitable for at least two centuries; yet since approximately 2009, short-term trends have ceased to deliver reliable returns. The paper examines roughly 100 liquid futures contracts from 1995-2025 alongside a CTA proxy, documenting this shift, and evaluates four candidate explanations -- capacity constraints, market electronification, CTA-order-flow regime changes, and microstructural mechanisms -- finding the first three inadequate based on timing, magnitude, or cross-sectional patterns. The key finding concerns volatility-normalized tick size: post-2008 trend PnL has collapsed on small-tick contracts across all signal horizons, while remaining essentially intact on large-tick ones. The authors propose a self-reinforcing feedback mechanism where trend signals generate directional trades whose market impact amplifies price movements, arguing that post-2008 high-frequency market-making, with its liquidity withdrawal ahead of predictable directional flow, disrupted this mechanism on small-tick contracts while leaving large-tick contracts relatively unaffected.
